Occupying the Old Boys Club: The Need for More Black Women in Tech

Wynta Alexander July 1 2020

3

That's the total percentage of computing - related jobs held by Black/African American women according to a 2017 report by the National Center for Women & Information Technology.

Since adolescence, I was always both intrigued and intimidated by computers. I was motivated by the unknown - and saw the opportunity to enroll in a tech skills course while in a Psychology master's program, I decided to finally take the leap into the world of technology.

Why Black/African American women should learning programming

  • On average, Black women receive 39% and 21% less pay than white men and women respectively even when the education attainment is similar. In layman's terms - Black women must possess more advantages than their non - Black peers to give them an edge in the workplace.

  • Learning code is a life skill that is a significant competitive advantage that can set Black women apart from their peers who are not as tech - savvy.

  • Learning code offers the opportunity to get ahead of the curve in the workplace. Technology is the language of the present and the future - it is immersed in our daily life and heavily relied on by society.

  • Allows for empowering tech endeavors - designing a website and/or starting an entrepreneurship. Although Black women are starting businesses faster than any racial group; despite only accounting for 14% of the U.S. female population, Black women represent 42% of net new - female owned businesses according to a 2019 report by American Express, only 4% of those businesses are tech - related start ups.

  • Can become a role model for other Black women who inspire to learn programming.

So, then why are Black/African American women being left behind?

  • Lack of exposure and participation in STEM enrichment programs beginning in youth and adolescence.

  • Lack of Black/African American role models and mentors in STEM can lead to discouragement in pursing STEM educational programs.

  • Lack of Black/African women in managerial/senior positions in the field of STEM.

Programs focusing on providing technology education to Black/African American girls

  • Black Girls Code - Educating Black girls in computer science between the ages of 7 - 17.
